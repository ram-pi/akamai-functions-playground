01 — reserved address ranges

Defined in RFC 6598 (2012), the 100.64.0.0/10 block is not an RFC 1918 range — it is Shared Address Space, a third category of non-routable IPv4 addresses. It was carved out specifically for Carrier-Grade NAT (CGNAT), the mechanism ISPs use to share a single public IPv4 address across hundreds of subscribers as the global address pool exhausted. Traffic in this range is never forwarded by Internet routers, but unlike RFC 1918 it is also not intended for general private networks — it sits in a grey zone between an ISP's infrastructure and the public internet.

Why a new block? If ISPs had used 10.x.x.x for CGNAT, collisions with customers' own RFC 1918 LANs would break end-to-end connectivity. 100.64.0.0/10 gives ISPs 4 million addresses that are globally reserved, won't appear in customer networks, and won't leak to the public internet — a clean boundary for the SP-to-CPE link.
🦎
Tailscale
WireGuard VPN
Tailscale assigns every enrolled device a stable 100.x.y.z address (a "Tailscale IP") from the 100.64.0.0/10 block. Devices reach each other directly over WireGuard tunnels — the overlay works regardless of whether the underlying network is RFC 1918, public, or even another CGNAT. The CGNAT block was chosen precisely because it doesn't overlap with RFC 1918, so Tailscale addresses won't clash with home or office LANs.
🐦
NetBird
WireGuard VPN
NetBird (open-source peer-to-peer VPN) also defaults to the 100.64.0.0/10 range for its WireGuard overlay. Each peer receives a /32 address within the block from the management plane. Admins can override to any private range, but the default mirrors Tailscale's reasoning: CGNAT space avoids RFC 1918 conflicts and is globally non-routable, making it ideal for zero-trust overlays deployed across heterogeneous environments.
🏭
ISP / CGNAT
Original use
The original intent: ISPs assign 100.64.x.x addresses to CPE (home router WAN interfaces). The ISP's CGNAT device translates those to a small pool of public IPs. Subscribers share public addresses via PAT. This is why addresses starting with 100.64–100.127 can show up in traceroute hops — they are SP infrastructure, not your LAN.
